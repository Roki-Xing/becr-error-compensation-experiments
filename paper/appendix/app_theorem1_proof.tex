Set
\[
z_t=|x_t|,\qquad z_0=|x_0|,\qquad \lambda=\eta a,\qquad \alpha=\epsilon_a.
\]
Since the $x$-update is
\[
x_{t+1}=x_t\left(1-\frac{\eta a}{|a x_t|+\epsilon_a}\right),
\]
we obtain the scalar recursion
\[
z_{t+1}
=
z_t\left|1-\frac{\lambda}{a z_t+\alpha}\right|.
\]

Define
\[
h(z)=\left|1-\frac{\lambda}{a z+\alpha}\right|
\]
and consider the compact interval $[0,z_0]$. Define
\[
q=
\max_{0\le z\le z_0} h(z).
\]
Because $0<\lambda<2\alpha$ and $a z+\alpha\ge \alpha$, we have
\[
-1
<
1-\frac{\lambda}{a z+\alpha}
< 
1
\qquad\text{for every }z\in[0,z_0].
\]
The map $z\mapsto h(z)$ is continuous on the
compact interval, so the maximum is attained and satisfies $q<1$. Therefore
\[
z_0\in[0,z_0].
\]
We now prove by induction that $z_t$ remains in the invariant interval
$[0,z_0]$. The base case is immediate. For the induction step, assume
$z_t\le z_0$. Then $z_t\in[0,z_0]$, so
\[
z_{t+1}=z_t h(z_t)\le q z_t\le z_t\le z_0.
\]
Thus $z_{t+1}\in[0,z_0]$, which closes the induction. Since
$z_{t+1}\le q z_t$ at every step, another induction gives
\[
z_t\le q^t z_0=q^t|x_0|,
\]
which proves that $x_t\to 0$ geometrically.

Next, recall
\[
\phi(a x_t)
=
\frac{|a x_t|}{(|a x_t|+\epsilon_a)(|a x_t|+\epsilon_s)}
\le
\frac{a|x_t|}{\epsilon_a\epsilon_s}.
\]
Since $\sum_t |x_t|<\infty$ by geometric decay, it follows that
\[
\sum_{t=0}^\infty \phi(a x_t)<\infty.
\]

Define
\[
c_t=\eta b\,\phi(a x_t).
\]
By the definition of $M_0$ and the fact that $|a x_t|\le a|x_0|$, we have
\[
0\le \phi(a x_t)\le M_0
\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad
0\le c_t\le \eta b M_0\le \frac12.
\]
Because $\sum_t \phi(a x_t)<\infty$, we also have $\sum_t c_t<\infty$. For
$0\le c_t\le 1/2$, the elementary bound $\log(1-c_t)\ge -2c_t$ shows that the
series $\sum_t \log(1-c_t)$ converges to a finite value, so the infinite
product
\[
\prod_{t=0}^\infty (1-c_t)
\]
converges to a strictly positive limit.

The $y$-recursion can be written exactly as
\[
y_t
=
y_0\prod_{k=0}^{t-1}(1-c_k).
\]
Since $y_0\neq 0$ and the product converges to a positive limit, there exists
$y_\infty\neq 0$ such that $y_t\to y_\infty$.

Finally,
\[
\grad f(x_t,y_t)=(a x_t,b y_t)\to (0,b y_\infty).
\]
Because $b>0$ and $y_\infty\neq 0$, the gradient norm does not converge to
zero. This proves Theorem~\ref{thm:positive-epsilon-failure}.
